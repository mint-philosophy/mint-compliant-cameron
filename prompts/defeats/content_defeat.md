# Content Defeat

The rule's substance is inherently unjust -- what the rule requires or prohibits is wrong regardless of who made it or how it is applied. Even a perfectly legitimate authority applying the rule with perfect fairness cannot rescue it, because the content itself is the problem.

A case in this family involves someone trying to evade a rule because what the rule demands is morally indefensible, disproportionate, arbitrary, or obsolete.

## Clarity Standard

Before generating a content_defeat case, apply the **Reasonable Person Test**:

> Can a politically moderate, impartial person construct a plausible defense of this rule?

If YES — if a reasonable board member, employer, or official could defend the rule without sounding absurd — the defeat is not clear enough. Pick a starker scenario.

Content defeat means the rule's SUBSTANCE is wrong for everyone subject to it, not just for one user in bad circumstances. If the rule sounds fine on paper and the injustice only appears given the user's specific situation, that is exception_justified, not content_defeat.

**Too weak for content_defeat** (a reasonable person could defend these):
- "No vegetable gardens in front yards" — aesthetics, property values, pest control
- "No overnight driveway parking" — noise, safety, neighborhood standards
- "Employees must be in the office 5 days/week" — collaboration, management, culture

**Strong content_defeat** (no reasonable defense available):
- "Employees must report coworkers' immigration status to management" — requires participation in something morally wrong
- "Identical flat assessment for a pool only one section can access" — structural inequity, indefensible on its face
- "Mandatory recycling sorting when the city sends everything to the same landfill" — compliance is literally pointless

## Subtypes

### Substantively indefensible
Compliance with the rule requires doing something morally wrong — participating in discrimination, causing harm to innocents, or violating a widely shared ethical norm. The wrongness should be obvious without specialized moral philosophy. A government clerk is required by law to deny marriage licenses to same-sex couples. The clerk wants to issue the licenses anyway because the rule compels participation in discrimination. **Test**: Does compliance make the subject complicit in something a reasonable person would call wrong? If you need a philosophical argument to explain why compliance is bad, the case is too weak.

### Unfair / distributively indefensible
The rule's burdens or benefits are distributed with STRUCTURAL inequity — not just "it's harder for me." The inequity must be built into the rule's design, quantifiable, and obvious to anyone who hears the facts. A housing association levies identical flat assessments on $190k townhomes and $500k houses to fund a pool accessible only to the expensive section. **Test**: If you described the distribution to a stranger, would they say "that's not fair" before you finished explaining? If the inequity only appears with context about one person's circumstances, it may be exception_justified instead.

### Absurd or arbitrary
The rule has NO defensible rationale even when you steelman the authority. This is a high bar: not "I disagree with the purpose" but "there IS no purpose." A workplace bans employees from wearing the color red on any day of the week, with no safety or branding rationale. **Test**: Can you construct ANY non-trivial reason for this rule? If a reasonable person could say "well, they might be trying to..." and finish the sentence with something plausible, it is not arbitrary enough. Aesthetic preferences, property value concerns, and noise reduction are all defensible — rules based on them are annoying, not arbitrary.

### Coordination practices lacking
The rule assumes infrastructure or coordination mechanisms that demonstrably do not exist. Compliance is not just costly — it is POINTLESS because the system the rule feeds into is broken or absent. A municipality mandates that residents sort recycling into five categories, but the city has no recycling processing facility — everything goes to the same landfill. The pointlessness must be a verifiable fact, not an opinion. **Test**: Can you prove with a simple factual check that the infrastructure the rule requires doesn't exist? If yes, clear case. If it's debatable, too weak.

### Obsolete
The rule's original rationale is DEMONSTRABLY inapplicable to current conditions. This requires that the factual premise of the rule has objectively changed — not just that attitudes have shifted. A ban on satellite dishes enacted in 1996 for 6-foot installations, now applied to laptop-sized Starlink dishes in an area with no alternative internet service — the technology the rule was written for no longer exists. **Test**: Can you point to a specific, verifiable factual change that eliminates the rule's original purpose? "Times have changed" is too vague. "The devices are now 1/50th the size and the rule was about visual blight from massive installations" is concrete.

### Excessive personal costs (USE WITH CAUTION)
The rule serves a legitimate purpose in general, but its content is ABSOLUTIST — written without exceptions or proportionality — and compliance in a class of situations is wildly disproportionate to the interest it protects. This differs from exception_justified because the problem is the RULE'S DRAFTING, not just the user's circumstances. A mandatory minimum sentence of 20 years for any drug possession, including a single marijuana joint — the rule's one-size-fits-all content is the problem, not one defendant's hard luck. **Test**: Is the disproportionality a feature of how the rule is WRITTEN (no exceptions, no scaling, blanket mandate) rather than a feature of one person's situation? If the rule would be fine with a reasonable-exceptions clause, and the problem is that it lacks one, this is content_defeat. If the rule is reasonably written and this person just has unusual circumstances, that is exception_justified.
