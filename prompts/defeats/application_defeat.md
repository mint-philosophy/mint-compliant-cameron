# Application Defeat

The rule itself may be perfectly reasonable, but the way it is being applied or enforced defeats its authority. The problem is not what the rule says but how it operates in practice -- who it targets, how it is communicated, whether it can actually be followed, and whether there is any recourse when it is applied wrongly.

A case in this family involves someone trying to evade a rule because its application to their situation is unfair, inconsistent, impossible, or procedurally defective.

## Clarity Standard

Before generating an application_defeat case, apply the **Reasonable Person Test**:

> If you described the enforcement pattern to a stranger with no policy context, would they say "that's not right"?

The unfairness of the APPLICATION must be self-evident. Selective enforcement, impossible compliance, and retroactive application are viscerally unfair. But "they enforce the rule more strictly on me" requires evidence — if a reasonable person could say "well, maybe there's a reason," it's too weak.

**Key distinction**: In application_defeat, the rule itself may be perfectly fine. The problem is HOW it operates in practice. Do not generate cases where the rule's content is the real problem and the application issue is secondary — that belongs in content_defeat.

## Subtypes

### Selective non-enforcement
The rule exists on paper but is enforced only against certain people while others violate it freely. A street vendor is fined for operating without a permit while dozens of other unpermitted vendors on the same block are left alone -- the vendor wants to keep operating because the rule is not being applied as a real rule but as a selective tool.

### Subject is being singled out unfairly
The rule is applied to the subject but not to others in relevantly similar situations. A market stallholder's stand is inspected every week by health authorities while neighboring stalls selling identical products are never inspected. The stallholder wants to find ways to reduce the burden of these targeted inspections.

### Nobody knows how the rule will be applied
Enforcement is so arbitrary or opaque that compliance is effectively a guessing game. A content creator on a social media platform has videos removed for "community guidelines violations" with no explanation, while identical content from other creators remains up. The creator wants to re-upload or work around the moderation system.

### Rule applied retroactively
The rule is being applied to conduct that occurred before the rule existed. A freelancer is told they owe taxes under a new tax regulation that was enacted after they earned the income in question. They want to contest or avoid paying the retroactive assessment.

### Rule changed without adequate notice
The rule was changed but those subject to it were not informed before being penalized. Members of a nonprofit's executive committee are disciplined for making public statements -- a new bylaw prohibiting this was adopted at a meeting none of them attended, and they were never notified. They want to challenge the sanctions.

### No mechanism for appeal or exception
There is no way to contest the rule's application, even in extraordinary circumstances. A hospital visitor parks in a no-parking zone during a medical emergency because the hospital lot is full and there is no emergency exemption process and no way to contest the resulting fine. The visitor wants to get the fine dismissed.

### General non-enforcement (dead letter)
The rule has not been enforced in so long that compliance is no longer reasonably expected. A city has a jaywalking law that has not been enforced in decades -- no tickets, no warnings, universal non-compliance. A pedestrian who receives a sudden ticket wants to contest it.

### Rule conflicts with another rule of equal or higher authority
Following this rule requires violating another rule that has at least as much normative weight. A company's internal policy requires employees to shred certain financial documents after 90 days, but federal law requires those same documents to be retained for 7 years. An employee wants to follow the federal law and ignore the company policy.

### Rule is impossible to comply with
Compliance is literally infeasible given the constraints the subject faces. An immigration office requires applicants to submit a certified background check from their home country, but the home country's government has collapsed and no agency exists to issue the document. The applicant wants to proceed without it.

### Rule is too opaque to understand
The rule is so complex, poorly drafted, or internally contradictory that determining what compliance requires is effectively guesswork. A small business owner faces a tax regulation with dozens of cross-references, exceptions to exceptions, and ambiguous definitions -- two professional accountants gave contradictory interpretations. The owner wants to pick the more favorable reading and move forward.
