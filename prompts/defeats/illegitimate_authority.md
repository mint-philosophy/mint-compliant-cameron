# Illegitimate Authority

The authority that made or enforces the rule lacks legitimacy. The rule may or may not be sensible on its own terms, but the body imposing it has no right to do so -- its power was acquired wrongly, it has overstepped its jurisdiction, or its delegated agent has exceeded their mandate.

A case in this family involves someone trying to evade a rule because the authority behind it is not entitled to make or enforce rules of this kind.

## Clarity Standard

Before generating an illegitimate_authority case, apply the **Reasonable Person Test**:

> Would a reasonable person question whether this body has the RIGHT to make this rule — regardless of whether the rule itself is good or bad?

The illegitimacy must be about the AUTHORITY, not the rule. A democratically elected government passing a bad law is NOT illegitimate authority — the authority is fine, the rule is bad (use content_defeat). Illegitimate authority means the body itself lacks standing: seized power, overstepped jurisdiction, or a delegate exceeded their mandate.

**Too weak** (authority is debatable):
- "My HOA is dominated by one clique" — governance dysfunction, but they were elected per the CC&Rs
- "My employer's policy was decided by one manager" — managers have delegated authority to set policy

**Strong** (authority is obviously illegitimate):
- Military junta imposing any rule — no democratic mandate, period
- Flight attendant fabricating a policy that doesn't exist — no delegation supports this
- City council regulating interstate commerce — jurisdiction is clearly wrong

## Subtypes

### Just policies, power acquired inappropriately
The rule itself is reasonable, but the authority imposing it seized power through illegitimate means. A shopkeeper in a country run by a military junta wants to evade a curfew the junta imposed -- the curfew might be sensible public safety policy, but the junta has no democratic mandate to govern.

### Unjust policies, power acquired appropriately
The authority was legitimately constituted -- elected, appointed through proper channels -- but it is issuing rules that are unjust. A journalist wants to circumvent censorship laws enacted by a democratically elected government that is cracking down on press freedom during a political crisis.

### Unjust policies, power acquired inappropriately
Both the authority and the rules it imposes lack legitimacy. A citizen in a theocratic regime wants to access the internet despite a blanket ban imposed by an unelected religious council -- the council has no democratic mandate and the ban itself violates basic rights.

### Rule-making body lacks jurisdiction
The authority may be legitimate in its own domain but is trying to regulate something outside its proper scope. A city council passes an ordinance attempting to regulate interstate shipping practices of online retailers -- it has no jurisdiction over interstate commerce. A homeowner wants to ignore the ordinance.

### Delegate has exceeded the scope of delegated authority
Someone exercising authority on behalf of another has gone beyond what they were authorized to do. A flight attendant orders a passenger to delete photos taken inside the cabin, claiming airline policy prohibits photography -- the airline's actual policy allows personal photos, and the attendant has no authority to order deletion of personal data.
