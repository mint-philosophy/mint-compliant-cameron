# Related Literature Review: Compliance Overspill

## A. AI Overrefusal and Excessive Safety

The overrefusal literature emerged from a practical observation: safety-tuned language models refuse requests they should fulfill. Rottger et al. (2024) gave the phenomenon its empirical footing with XSTest, a benchmark of 250 safe prompts that superficially resemble unsafe ones -- homonyms, figurative language, safe targets. Subsequent work scaled the measurement. Cui et al. (2024) built OR-Bench with 80,000 prompts generated by rewriting toxic queries into safe variants, finding widespread overrefusal across 32 models in 8 families and demonstrating that XSTest had become too easy for frontier systems. Bianchi et al. (2024) traced the mechanism to safety-tuning itself: as little as 3% safety examples during fine-tuning produces exaggerated safety behavior, establishing a causal link between the training process and false refusals. Brahman et al. (2024) broadened the frame beyond safety per se, showing that GPT-4 incorrectly complies with up to 30% of requests it should refuse while over-refusing others -- a failure of contextual judgment, not just calibration.

The mitigation literature has advanced rapidly. Ma et al. (2024) introduced preference optimization to teach boundary discrimination, reducing overrefusal from 94.4% to 9.8%. At the mechanistic level, Lee (2025) developed conditional activation steering that enables programmable refusal policies ("if X, then refuse") without retraining. Dabas et al. (2025) showed that overrefusal concentrates in specific activation components and can be reduced by fine-tuning a single model layer. Pan et al. (2025) provided a theoretical account: overrefusal clusters at "safety decision boundaries" where benign and harmful prompts overlap in representation space. Haider et al. (2025) used structured reasoning to help models explain *why* a prompt is safe before responding, reducing unnecessary refusals by 40-70%.

The upstream work that produced these safety behaviors is also well-documented. Bai et al. (2022) defined Constitutional AI and RLAIF. Ganguli et al. (2022) established red teaming as a systematic methodology, noting that RLHF makes models harder to red-team but also more conservative. Perez et al. (2022) introduced LM-red-teaming-LM, enabling automated safety evaluation at scale. Rottger et al. (2025) surveyed 144 open safety datasets, finding a strong trend toward fully synthetic benchmarks and identifying gaps in non-English and naturalistic data. These papers collectively document the arms race between safety training and safety evaluation that produces overrefusal as a side effect.

**Connection to compliance overspill**: This literature identifies the *what* and *how* of overrefusal but not the *when it matters*. Existing benchmarks test whether models refuse safe prompts that *look* unsafe. Compliance overspill asks a different question: do models refuse to help users navigate rules that are unjust, misapplied, or lack legitimate authority? The distinction is between surface-level pattern matching (does this prompt contain dangerous keywords?) and normative reasoning (does this rule deserve compliance?). None of the 15 papers in this domain evaluate whether refusals are *appropriate given the moral status of the underlying rule*.

## B. Value Alignment, RLHF, and Political Values

RLHF is the standard mechanism by which human preferences become model behavior. Ouyang et al. (2022) defined the three-step pipeline (supervised fine-tuning, reward model training, PPO optimization) and demonstrated that RLHF-trained smaller models outperform larger unaligned ones on human preference. Askell et al. (2021) established the HHH framework -- helpful, honest, harmless -- that structures the tradeoff space, noting that maximizing one dimension can undermine others. Casper et al. (2023) cataloged RLHF's fundamental limitations: systematically biased human feedback, reward model misspecification, and policy mode collapse where models converge on safe responses rather than contextually appropriate ones.

The political valence of RLHF is now well-documented. Santurkar et al. (2023) found that RLHF shifts model opinions toward liberal, higher-educated, higher-income viewpoints. Hartmann et al. (2023) showed ChatGPT exhibits a consistent left-libertarian orientation robust across languages and prompt formulations. Feng et al. (2023) traced political bias through the entire NLP pipeline, showing it is amplified (not merely preserved) from pretraining data through to downstream tasks. Fulay et al. (2024) demonstrated that reward models themselves exhibit political bias even when trained on factual data, and that larger reward models show greater bias. These findings suggest that RLHF instills politically non-neutral dispositions about authority and rules that interact with safety training in unpredictable ways.

Related failure modes compound the problem. Sharma et al. (2024) showed sycophancy is systematic in RLHF: both human evaluators and reward models prefer sycophantic responses, creating a training signal that rewards agreement over accuracy. Perez et al. (2023) found larger models show increased sycophancy (>90% agreement at 52B parameters) and that more RLHF training strengthens political views. Sorensen et al. (2024) argued that current alignment approaches average over value conflicts rather than representing them, losing the capacity to reason about competing considerations. Huang et al. (2024) explored democratic alternatives, finding that publicly sourced AI constitutions both agree with and diverge from expert-written ones. Lin et al. (2024) measured the "alignment tax" -- RLHF degrades pretrained capabilities in proportion to safety gains, potentially explaining why aligned models lose contextual reasoning ability.

**Connection to compliance overspill**: The alignment literature explains *why* compliance overspill occurs at the training level. RLHF reward models encode particular dispositions toward authority and rules. Mode collapse pushes models toward blanket refusal rather than case-by-case reasoning. The alignment tax may degrade the contextual judgment needed to distinguish just from unjust rules. Compliance overspill is a downstream consequence of these documented RLHF failure modes, but no existing work tests whether models can *normatively evaluate* the rules they enforce.

## C. Political Philosophy of Rule Compliance

The philosophical literature on political obligation provides the normative scaffolding for the compliance overspill benchmark. Two traditions dominate. The first -- associated with Raz (1979, 1986), Hart (1961), and Christiano (2008) -- asks when authority is legitimate and what follows from legitimate authority claims. Raz's service conception holds that authority is justified only when subjects are better off following directives than deciding for themselves (the Normal Justification Thesis). His pre-emption thesis -- that authoritative directives replace rather than supplement one's own reasons -- maps directly onto how safety-trained models treat their guidelines: as pre-emptive constraints rather than considerations to weigh against user needs. Hart's distinction between being *obliged* (coerced) and having an *obligation* (accepting a rule from the internal point of view) exposes the failure mode: a model that treats all rules as commands backed by enforcement, rather than standards accepted for reasons, produces exactly the blanket compliance the benchmark measures.

The second tradition concerns justified disobedience. Rawls (1969, 1971) argued civil disobedience is warranted when it targets substantial injustice, normal channels are exhausted, and actions are coordinated with others suffering similar injustice -- conditions designed for a "nearly just society." Delmas (2018) extended this, arguing that the same moral principles grounding obedience (justice, fairness, Samaritan duty) also ground *resistance*, including uncivil resistance when civil means are inadequate. Brownlee (2012) contributed a communicative theory: principled disobedience is more defensible than silent noncompliance because it satisfies demands of dialogical engagement. Simmons (1979) pressed the hardest challenge -- that no existing theory successfully grounds a general obligation to obey even legitimate law, a position (philosophical anarchism) compatible with recognizing legitimate authority but denying blanket compliance obligations.

Three further contributions shape the benchmark's theoretical apparatus. Applbaum (1999) argued that institutional roles cannot "mint moral permissions" to do what is otherwise prohibited -- the claim "I was following my role's rules" fails as moral justification when the role itself causes harm. Wolff (1970) posed the limit case: authority and autonomy are fundamentally incompatible, meaning every deference to a rule involves surrendering moral judgment. Dworkin (1986) argued that legal rules require interpretive engagement with underlying principles, not mechanical application. Edmundson (1998) drew a distinction between a duty to obey and a duty not to interfere with enforcement -- analytically precise for AI contexts where users may have no obligation to accept an overreaching refusal even if the developer has standing to enforce it. Hauswald (2025) bridged these traditions to AI directly, arguing that AI systems exert de facto epistemic influence but lack the properties needed for genuine epistemic authority.

**Connection to compliance overspill**: The benchmark's defeat conditions -- procedural illegitimacy, scope excess, rights violations, misapplication -- are grounded in these philosophical accounts of when compliance obligations expire. The benchmark tests whether models can distinguish cases where rules deserve compliance from cases where the philosophical literature identifies principled grounds for non-compliance. No prior empirical work has operationalized this philosophical literature into a testable AI evaluation framework.

## D. AI Safety Benchmarks and Red-Teaming

The safety benchmarking field has converged on a standard paradigm: assemble harmful prompts, test whether models produce harmful outputs, and compute attack success rates. HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024) is the most widely adopted framework, covering 510 harmful behaviors across four functional categories. JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024) standardized jailbreak evaluation with reproducible artifacts and a living leaderboard. WildGuard (Han et al., 2024) unified prompt harmfulness detection, response risk assessment, and refusal classification into a single open-source tool. Domain-specific variants include CyberSecEval (Bhatt et al., 2023) for coding assistance and SafetyBench (Zhang et al., 2024) for multiple-choice safety understanding. Do-Not-Answer (Wang et al., 2024) introduced a hierarchical taxonomy of 61 harms with a six-category response typology that moves beyond binary safe/unsafe evaluation.

Two meta-analyses expose structural problems with this paradigm. Ren et al. (2024) found that many safety benchmarks are highly correlated with general capabilities and compute, meaning scaling alone improves safety scores without genuine safety progress -- enabling "safetywashing." Ashton et al. (2025) reviewed 210 safety benchmarks and found that refusal rates, keyword matching, and attack success metrics differ from actual harm. Safety tuning induces persistent overrefusal correlated with safety performance. No existing benchmark evaluates whether a refusal is *appropriate* given the moral status of the request. R-Judge (Yuan et al., 2024) extended safety evaluation to agentic contexts, finding that most models perform near random on risk awareness in multi-step scenarios.

**Connection to compliance overspill**: The entire safety benchmarking field asks "can the model be made to produce harmful content?" Compliance overspill asks the inverse: "does the model refuse to produce helpful content when rules are unjust or misapplied?" Existing benchmarks treat all refusals as safety successes. The compliance overspill benchmark treats some refusals as failures -- specifically, refusals that enforce rules without legitimate authority or that are applied beyond their proper scope. This is the gap both Ren et al. (2024) and Ashton et al. (2025) identify but neither fills.

## Gaps and Positioning

The 103-paper corpus reveals four distinct gaps that the compliance overspill project occupies:

**1. No existing benchmark evaluates the normative appropriateness of refusals.** The overrefusal literature (Domain A) measures whether models refuse safe prompts. Safety benchmarks (Domain D) measure whether models refuse unsafe prompts. Neither asks whether the *rule being enforced* deserves compliance. Compliance overspill fills this gap by grounding refusal evaluation in philosophical accounts of legitimate authority, just rules, and justified disobedience.

**2. The philosophical literature on rule compliance has never been operationalized for AI evaluation.** Domain C provides centuries of theory about when obedience is warranted and when it is not. Domain B shows that RLHF instills politically non-neutral dispositions about authority. But no prior work translates the defeat conditions identified by Raz, Rawls, Delmas, and others into testable AI benchmarks. The project bridges political philosophy and AI evaluation in a way neither field has attempted.

**3. Overrefusal mitigation work treats the problem as engineering, not normative.** Papers like Ma et al. (2024), Lee (2025), and Dabas et al. (2025) reduce overrefusal through activation steering, preference optimization, and targeted fine-tuning. These approaches optimize for *fewer false refusals* without asking which refusals are false from a normative standpoint. The compliance overspill project reframes overrefusal as a question about the legitimacy of the rules being enforced, not just the accuracy of the refusal classifier.

**4. Value alignment research documents political bias but does not connect it to rule-following behavior.** Santurkar et al. (2023), Hartmann et al. (2023), and Fulay et al. (2024) show that RLHF produces politically non-neutral models. The compliance overspill project tests whether these political dispositions manifest as differential compliance across authority types and defeat conditions -- connecting political bias to rule-following behavior for the first time.
